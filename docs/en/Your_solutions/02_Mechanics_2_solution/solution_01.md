## Pendulum Calculations: Earth vs. Moon

The period of a simple pendulum is determined by its length and the local acceleration due to gravity. The relationship is defined by the following formula:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$



---

### 1. Period of the Pendulum on the Moon

To find the period on the Moon ($T_M$) based on the period on Earth ($T_E$), we use the ratio of the two gravitational constants. Since the length ($L$) of the pendulum is constant:

$$\frac{T_M}{T_E} = \sqrt{\frac{g_E}{g_M}}$$

Given that the Moon's gravity ($g_M$) is approximately $1/6$ of Earth's gravity ($g_E$):

* **Earth Period ($T_E$):** 4 seconds
* **Gravity Ratio ($g_E / g_M$):** 6

**Calculation:**
$$T_M = 4 \cdot \sqrt{6}$$
$$T_M \approx 4 \cdot 2.449$$
$$T_M \approx 9.80 \text{ seconds}$$

On the Moon, the pendulum would swing much slower, taking approximately **9.80 seconds** to complete one full cycle.

---

### 2. Required Length for a 1-Second Period on Earth

To find the specific length ($L$) required for a target period ($T$), we rearrange the formula to solve for $L$:

$$L = g \left( \frac{T}{2\pi} \right)^2$$

**Given Values:**
* **Target Period ($T$):** 1 second
* **Earth's Gravity ($g$):** $9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$

**Calculation:**
$$L = 9.81 \cdot \left( \frac{1}{2\pi} \right)^2$$
$$L = \frac{9.81}{4\pi^2}$$
$$L \approx 0.248 \text{ meters}$$

To have a period of exactly 1 second on Earth, the pendulum must be approximately **0.248 meters (or 24.8 cm)** long.
