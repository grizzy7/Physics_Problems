
To find the speed of the combined mass just after the collision, we need to break the problem into two phases: the block sliding down the track and the subsequent collision.



### Phase 1: Block 1 Slides Down the Track
Since the track is frictionless, we can use the **Law of Conservation of Mechanical Energy**. The gravitational potential energy at the height $h$ is converted entirely into kinetic energy at the bottom.

The equation is:
$$m_1gh = \frac{1}{2} m_1 v_1^2$$

Where:
* $m_1 = 0.5 \text{ kg}$
* $g \approx 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$
* $h = 3.0 \text{ m}$
* $v_1$ is the velocity of the first block at the bottom.

Solving for $v_1$:
$$v_1 = \sqrt{2gh}$$
$$v_1 = \sqrt{2 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 3.0} \approx 7.67 \text{ m/s}$$

### Phase 2: The Inelastic Collision
At the bottom, the 0.5 kg block ($m_1$) collides and sticks to the 1.5 kg block ($m_2$). Because they stick together, this is a **perfectly inelastic collision**. While kinetic energy is not conserved in this phase, **linear momentum** is.

The equation for conservation of momentum is:
$$m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2) v_f$$

Where:
* $m_1 = 0.5 \text{ kg}$
* $v_1 = 7.67 \text{ m/s}$
* $m_2 = 1.5 \text{ kg}$
* $v_2 = 0 \text{ m/s}$ (initially at rest)
* $v_f$ is the final velocity of the combined mass.

Plugging in the values:
$$(0.5 \cdot 7.67) + (1.5 \cdot 0) = (0.5 + 1.5) \cdot v_f$$
$$3.835 = 2.0 \cdot v_f$$

Solving for $v_f$:
$$v_f = \frac{3.835}{2.0} \approx 1.92 \text{ m/s}$$

**Final Answer:**
The speed of the combined mass just after the collision is approximately **$1.92 \text{ m/s}$**.
