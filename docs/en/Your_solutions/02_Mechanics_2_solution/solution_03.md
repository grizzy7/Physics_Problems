

To find the speed of the pendulum bob at the bottom of its swing, we can use the **principle of conservation of mechanical energy**. We assume there is no air resistance or friction at the pivot.

### 1. Identify the Energy at the Release Point
At the moment of release, the bob is at its maximum height $h$ relative to the bottom of the swing. All of its energy is stored as gravitational potential energy ($PE$):
$$PE = mgh$$
Where:
* $m$ is the mass of the bob.
* $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity ($\approx 9.81 \, \text{m/s}^2$).
* $h$ is the vertical height above the lowest point.

### 2. Calculate the Vertical Height $h$
From the geometry of the pendulum, the vertical height $h$ can be determined using the length of the string $L$ and the release angle $\theta$:
$$h = L - L \cos(\theta) = L(1 - \cos(\theta))$$

### 3. Identify the Energy at the Bottom
At the bottom of the swing, all the potential energy has been converted into kinetic energy ($KE$):
$$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
Where $v$ is the speed of the bob.

### 4. Solve for Speed $v$
By conservation of energy, $PE_{top} = KE_{bottom}$:
$$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
$$gh = \frac{1}{2}v^2$$
$$v = \sqrt{2gh}$$

Substituting the expression for $h$:
$$v = \sqrt{2gL(1 - \cos(\theta))}$$

### 5. Numerical Calculation
Given:
* $L = 1.0 \, \text{m}$
* $\theta = 15^\circ$
* $g = 9.81 \, \text{m/s}^2$

First, calculate $h$:
$$h = 1.0 \times (1 - \cos(15^\circ))$$
$$h \approx 1.0 \times (1 - 0.9659)$$
$$h \approx 0.0341 \, \text{m}$$

Now, calculate $v$:
$$v = \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 0.0341}$$
$$v = \sqrt{0.6685}$$
$$v \approx 0.818 \, \text{m/s}$$

**Final Answer:**
The speed of the pendulum bob at the bottom of its swing is approximately **$0.818 \, \text{m/s}$**.
