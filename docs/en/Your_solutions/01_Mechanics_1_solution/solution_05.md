To cross the river directly north, the boat must head slightly upstream (to the west) to counteract the river's eastward flow. This is a classic **vector addition** problem where the resultant velocity vector must point exactly North.

### 1. Finding the Heading Direction (Angle)
Let $V_b = 5\text{ m/s}$ be the speed of the boat in still water and $V_r = 2\text{ m/s}$ be the speed of the river current. To travel straight across, the horizontal (east-west) component of the boat's velocity must perfectly cancel out the river's flow.

We can visualize this as a right-angled triangle where:
* The **hypotenuse** is the boat's speed relative to the water ($5\text{ m/s}$).
* The **opposite side** is the river's speed ($2\text{ m/s}$).
* The **angle $\theta$** is measured from the North (vertical) line toward the West.



Using trigonometry:
$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} = \frac{V_r}{V_b}$$
$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{2}{5} = 0.4$$
$$\theta = \arcsin(0.4) \approx 23.58^\circ$$

**Result:** The boat should head at an angle of **$23.58^\circ$ West of North**.

---

### 2. Calculating the Time to Cross
To find the time, we first need the boat's **effective speed** directly across the river (the Northward component, $V_y$). We can find this using the Pythagorean theorem:

$$V_y = \sqrt{V_b^2 - V_r^2}$$
$$V_y = \sqrt{5^2 - 2^2} = \sqrt{25 - 4} = \sqrt{21}$$
$$V_y \approx 4.583\text{ m/s}$$

Now, use the formula $t = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}}$:
$$t = \frac{200\text{ m}}{\sqrt{21}\text{ m/s}}$$
$$t \approx \frac{200}{4.583} \approx 43.64\text{ seconds}$$

 It will take approximately **$43.64$ seconds** to cross the river.
